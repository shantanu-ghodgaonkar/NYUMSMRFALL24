Overview

Smart automation systems (e.g., driverless cars, domestic/warehouse mobile robots, intelligent transportation systems, robotic construction machines, etc.) need to understand both their own poses and their surroundings to fulfill tasks safely, accurately, and efficiently. This requires an intelligent extraction of both geometric and semantic information from sensory input (e.g., cameras/LIDARs). This course aims to combine the established theories of geometric vision and the recent progress in pattern recognition in the context of robotic/intelligent systems. Students will study and practice the basic theories of computer vision through relevant applications, e.g., pose estimation of a robotic agent from onboard cameras, 3D reconstruction for map creation, object detection/segmentation for obstacle avoidance, tracking for target following, place recognition from images when GPS is unreliable, and so on.

Prerequisites

This course is designed to be self-contained as much as possible for engineering graduate students with a reasonable knowledge of linear algebra, probability theory, and calculus. A basic understanding of image processing and machine learning will be helpful. Students should be familiar and comfortable with Python for homework and course projects. Note: This is NOT a basic programming/software course, so we do not offer a debugging service, except for basic environment setup.

Course Objectives

By the end of this course students should be able to:

  • Describe robot perception models/algorithms and their applicable problem types.
  • Compare assumptions in each model/algorithm and their strengths and weaknesses.
  • Determine appropriate robot perception models/algorithms for a given problem.
  • Formulate a robot perception problem formally.
  • Solve the formulated problem with programming tools.

On the Use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools

In this course, AI tools can be used to generate ideas, clean up your text, and catch grammatical errors, subject to the following rules:

  • Citation: If you incorporate AI-generated content into your work, you must cite it appropriately. This ensures academic integrity and allows for transparency in your learning process. I ask that any and all AI-generated text be highlighted or typed in a different color.  A citation with a sharable link may read as follows: ChatGPT-4. (2024, August). Title of the Chat. Link: https://chatgpt.com/share/XXXXXX.
  • Verification: You must personally verify all material submitted by you. You are cautioned that AI often generates wrong answers, and that you are responsible for all aspects of your work.
  • Comprehension: Your submission must demonstrate a clear understanding of the logic and intellectual merit underpinning your work.  I reserve the right to question you orally on anything that you submit. You should be able to explain and reproduce the work without AI assistance.
  • The use of AI tools is strictly prohibited for in-class quizzes (plus you won’t have time to do so in our quizzes). These assessments are designed to evaluate your individual understanding and ability to apply the concepts learned in class. It is vital for your professional development to be proficient and autonomous in problem-solving and recalling certain information without resorting to AI.

Finally, AI should never be used to replace the effort required for you to learn.  If you do, you would have wasted your time and money, and a computer will eventually replace you in the workplace.

Academic Honesty

Integrity, which starts in school, is a key ingredient in becoming a professional. Academic Honesty is assumed of all students; a zero-tolerance policy will be enforced with respect to cheating, which is defined to include any act to misrepresent someone else’s work as your own. While we encourage free discussion among students, anyone caught cheating (direct copying of quiz answers, source codes, homework solution texts, and/or project reports) will automatically fail this class. Please refer to the NYU Tandon School of Engineering code of conduct available at engineering.nyu.edu/student-code-conduct for further details. NYU’s academic integrity policy has been updated to incorporate the emergence of Generative AI tools. In particular, the policy states that plagiarism is a form of fraud. It involves presenting work without adequate acknowledgment of its source (e.g., another person, your own earlier work, or AI tools, etc.), as though it were one’s own current work.
